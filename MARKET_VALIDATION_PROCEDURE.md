# STANDARDIZED MARKET VALIDATION & VIABILITY PROCEDURE
## For GHOSTWRITR Promise Stage

**Purpose:** Ensure every book promise is grounded in real-world market evidence, not assumptions. This procedure integrates validation at the promise stage and gates progression to outlining.

**Output:** Market validation document that becomes foundation for all downstream book decisions (audience, positioning, content structure)

---

## PHASE 1: PROBLEM VALIDATION (Week 1)

### Step 1.1: Define Core Problem (One Sentence)

**Task:** Distill the book's core problem to one clear statement that a target reader would recognize immediately.

**Process:**
- Read the current promise statement
- Extract the central problem being solved
- Remove all adjectives and positioning language
- Test: Can a 10-year-old understand it?

**Output Example:**
- ❌ "Lab professionals struggle to transition from technical mastery to effective leadership"
- ✓ "Lab leaders can't delegate without their lab breaking"

**Validation Test:**
```
Problem Statement:
[INSERT ONE SENTENCE]

Would your target customer immediately recognize this as THEIR problem? (Yes/No)
If no, refine until they would.
```

### Step 1.2: Assess Problem Frequency

**Task:** Determine HOW OFTEN this problem occurs in the target market.

**Research Sources:**
- Academic papers (search: "[domain] pain points" OR "[role] challenges")
- Industry reports
- Surveys (search: "[domain] state of the industry")
- News articles covering the problem
- Reddit/forum discussions in that community

**Questions to Answer:**
1. What percentage of target audience experiences this problem?
2. What percentage experience it FREQUENTLY (weekly, monthly)?
3. Is it described as systemic or isolated?

**Output:**
```
Frequency Score: 1-5
- 5 = 80%+ of audience experiences this frequently
- 3 = 40-60% of audience experiences this
- 1 = Niche problem (5-20% experience it)

Evidence:
[List 3-5 sources proving frequency]

Research Quality: [High/Medium/Low]
[Note any gaps in evidence]
```

### Step 1.3: Assess Problem Severity

**Task:** Quantify the pain (time, cost, stress, risk, career impact).

**Research Sources:**
- Time tracking studies (how many hours spent on problem?)
- Financial impact (money lost, cost of not solving)
- Stress/burnout research
- Career impact (publications, advancement blocked)
- Safety/risk implications

**Questions to Answer:**
1. How much TIME does this problem consume? (hours/week, hours/year)
2. What is the FINANCIAL cost? (salary impact, productivity loss, opportunity cost)
3. What STRESS/EMOTIONAL cost? (burnout, anxiety, frustration)
4. What CAREER impact? (promotions blocked, publications delayed, reputation damage)

**Output:**
```
Time Impact: [X hours/week or X hours/year]
Financial Impact: [X dollars per year]
Stress Impact: [1-10 severity scale]
Career Impact: [1-10 severity scale]

Example Evidence: "[Source] shows X hours/week is typical"

Problem Severity Score: 1-5
- 5 = Severe impact across multiple dimensions (time, money, career)
- 3 = Moderate impact, affecting daily work
- 1 = Minor annoyance, people live with it

Most painful dimension: [time/money/stress/career]
```

### Step 1.4: Assess Active Problem-Solving Behavior

**Task:** Is the target audience ALREADY trying to solve this? (If not, they won't buy a book about it)

**Research Sources:**
- How much are organizations spending on solutions?
- Are there existing courses, training, books in this space?
- Do conferences have tracks dedicated to this?
- Are there consultants and coaches specializing in this?

**Questions to Answer:**
1. Do organizations invest in training/solutions for this problem?
2. Are multiple books/courses already published on this topic?
3. Do universities/companies offer workshops or certification?
4. Are there coaches or consultants billing for this?

**Output:**
```
Existing Solutions in Market:
1. [Type]: [Examples] - Cost: $X - Adoption rate: X%
2. [Type]: [Examples] - Cost: $X - Adoption rate: X%
3. [Type]: [Examples] - Cost: $X - Adoption rate: X%

Market Size of Existing Solutions: $XXX million
Growth rate: X% year-over-year

Conclusion: Is target audience ACTIVELY seeking solutions?
- Yes, proven by $X+ market
- Somewhat, X% have paid for solutions
- No, problem exists but not actively solved

Problem Validation Score: 1-5
- 5 = Systemic, severe, organizations spending $M+ annually
- 3 = Real but underfunded, small training market
- 1 = Hypothetical problem, no active market
```

## PHASE 2: AUDIENCE & ACCESS (Week 2)

### Step 2.1: Identify Most Likely Buyer

**Task:** Narrow from "target audience" to the ACTUAL person who will buy this book.

**Process:**
1. Who experiences the problem most acutely?
2. Who has money/authority to buy books?
3. Who has time to read?
4. Who is motivated to solve this problem?

**Output:**
```
Primary Buyer Persona:
- Job Title: [exact title used in industry]
- Responsibility: [what do they do daily?]
- Budget: [do they control budget? Size?]
- Authority: [can they approve purchases?]
- Pain: [how does THIS problem affect THEM specifically?]
- Motivation: [why would THEY buy a book about this?]

Secondary Buyer: [if different from reader]
Tertiary Buyer: [if applicable]

Reality Check: Is your "primary buyer" someone who actually buys books?
Or someone who reads books bought by others?
```

### Step 2.2: Assess How This Audience Currently LEARNS

**Task:** Where do they get training/education? Books, courses, peers, conferences?

**Research Sources:**
- Survey: "Where do professionals in this field learn?"
- Company websites (what training do they offer?)
- Conference attendance data
- Book/course recommendation boards
- Professional associations

**Questions to Answer:**
1. Do they read books? (What kind? How often?)
2. Do they take courses? (Online, in-person, self-paced?)
3. Do they attend conferences/workshops?
4. Do they learn from peers/mentors?
5. Do they consume short-form content? (Articles, podcasts, videos)

**Output:**
```
Learning Behavior Profile:

Books:
- Read professional/business books? (Yes/No)
- Frequency: [X per year]
- Format: [Hardcover/eBook/Audiobook]
- Where buy: [Amazon/Publisher/Bookstore/Library]
- Completion rate: [X% of books purchased are finished]

Courses/Training:
- Participate in professional training? (Yes/No)
- Format preferred: [Online/In-person/Certification]
- Investment: [$ per year on learning]

Conferences:
- Attend industry conferences? (Yes/No)
- Frequency: [X per year]

Peer Learning:
- Have mentors/coaches? (Yes/No)
- Join learning groups? (Yes/No)

Short-Form:
- Listen to podcasts? (Yes/No)
- Read articles/journals? (Yes/No)
- Watch videos? (Yes/No)

Primary Learning Channel: [books/courses/conferences/peers/articles]
Secondary: [second most used]

Book Readership Likelihood: 1-5
- 5 = Read 5+ professional books per year, high completion
- 3 = Read 2-3 per year, moderate completion
- 1 = Rarely read, prefer other formats
```

### Step 2.3: Assess Accessibility (How Will You REACH Them?)

**Task:** Can you actually get this book in front of your buyer?

**Research:**
- How do they discover books?
- Do they follow specific communities/platforms?
- Are there trusted advisors/influencers in their space?
- Do they actively search for solutions?

**Output:**
```
Distribution Channels Available:

Direct:
- Will they search for this on Amazon? (Yes/No/Maybe)
- Are there newsletters/communities they follow? (Which ones?)
- Will they attend conferences where you can sell? (Which?)

Indirect:
- Will universities/companies bulk-buy this? (Which?)
- Are there influencers/advisors who could recommend? (Who?)
- Can you partner with platforms in this space? (Which?)

Accessibility Score: 1-5
- 5 = Multiple clear channels (e.g., they follow 5+ relevant communities)
- 3 = Some channels but require effort
- 1 = Hard to reach, no obvious channels

Biggest accessibility challenge: [What's the bottleneck?]

Recommended distribution strategy: [Top 2-3 channels to focus on]
```

**Task:** What books or solutions already exist that promise to solve this problem?

**Research Process:**

2. Search Google Scholar: "[problem] [domain] research"
3. Ask in communities: "What books do people recommend for [problem]?"
4. Check publisher websites: (O'Reilly, Harvard, academic publishers)
5. Look for courses: Udemy, Coursera, MasterClass, corporate training

**Output:**
```
Direct Competitors (Books solving same problem, same audience):
1. [Title] by [Author], [Year], [Publisher]
   - Promise: [What does it promise?]

   - Assessment: [Strengths / Weaknesses]

2. [Title]...
3. [Title]...
4. [Title]...
5. [Title]...

Indirect Competitors (Solving same problem, broader audience):

```

### Step 3.2: Analyze Each Competitor

**For each major competitor, assess:**

```

```

**Output:**

## PHASE 4: DIFFERENTIATION ANALYSIS (Week 3)

### Step 4.1: Define Our Unique Positioning

**Task:** What makes THIS book different from competitors?

**Process:**
1. What is our specific promise? (Word for word from current promise statement)
2. What do competitors promise?
3. What's genuinely different?
4. Is that difference MEANINGFUL?

**Output:**
```
Our Promise:
"[Exact promise statement]"

Competitor Promises:
1. [Book]: "[Promise]"
2. [Book]: "[Promise]"
3. [Book]: "[Promise]"

Our Differentiation (One Sentence):
"Unlike [competitors who promise X], we [specific thing that's different]"

Example:
- ❌ "Unlike generic leadership books, we focus on delegation"
  (Too broad, they do too)
- ❌ "Unlike other books, we provide practical frameworks"
  (Every book claims this)
- ✓ "Unlike leadership books that teach soft skills, we restructure lab operations so delegation actually works—redesigning workflows, not just mindsets"
  (Specific, different, meaningful)

Test: Can a reader explain this difference in one sentence?
If not, refine until they can.
```

### Step 4.2: Is This Differentiation REAL?

**Questions:**
1. Can we actually deliver on this difference?
2. Is it based on real experience or re-packaging?
3. Can we prove it with examples/data?
4. Would readers immediately understand why it's better?

**Output:**
```
Differentiation Reality Check:

Claim: [Our differentiation statement]

Can we deliver? (Yes/No/Partly)
- What evidence do we have? [Examples, data, research]
- What would we need to add to prove this? [Case studies, data, tools]

Is it real or repackaging?
- [Assessment]

Would a reader immediately get why this is better?
- [Assessment]

Risk if differentiation is weak:
"If readers can't see why our book is different, they'll buy [competitor] instead"

Differentiation Strength Score: 1-5
- 5 = Clear, defensible, meaningful difference
- 3 = Some difference but not obvious
- 1 = Seems like repackaging of existing approaches
```

## PHASE 5: PRACTICAL VALUE & USABILITY (Week 4)

### Step 5.1: Will Readers Actually USE This?

**Task:** Map what a reader would DO differently after reading.

**Process:**
1. Pick your primary persona
2. Imagine them reading your book
3. What specific actions would they take?
4. By when? (1 week? 1 month?)
5. What would actually change?

**Output:**
```
Primary Reader Scenario:

Reader: [Name/role of primary persona]
Current Situation: [Their pain in detail]

After reading your book, they would:
- Week 1: [Specific action 1]
- Week 2: [Specific action 2]
- Week 3: [Specific action 3]
- Week 4: [Specific action 4]

By Month 2, they would have:
- [Specific outcome 1]
- [Specific outcome 2]
- [Measurable result?]

Reality Check: Is this realistic?
- Will they actually DO these things? [Yes/No/With help]
- What could block them? [Organizational barriers? Time?]
- What do they need from the book? [Tools? Permission? Framework?]

Usability Score: 1-5
- 5 = Readers can apply something concrete within 1 week
- 3 = Readers need 4-6 weeks to see results
- 1 = Vague guidance, hard to translate to action
```

### Step 5.2: What Tools/Frameworks Does the Book Provide?

**Output:**
```
Concrete Tools in the Book:

Tool/Framework Name:
- What is it? [Description]
- How is it used? [Step by step]
- Would reader use this? [Yes/No/Maybe]
- Can they use it immediately? [Yes/No]

Tool 2:
Tool 3:
Tool 4:
Tool 5:

Assessment:
- Are these tools SPECIFIC enough to use? (Or abstract?)
- Are there TEMPLATES/WORKSHEETS or just descriptions?
- Can reader apply without additional help?

Tool Completeness Score: 1-5
- 5 = Specific, templated, immediately applicable
- 3 = Useful but requires some interpretation
- 1 = Abstract, needs coaching to implement
```
